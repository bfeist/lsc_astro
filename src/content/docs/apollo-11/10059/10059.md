## **Introduction**

10059 is a coherent glass-matrix regolith breccia, with composition that matches the local soil (10084). It has a number of orange glass beads of supposed volcanic origin.

## **Petrography**

Simon et al. (1984) included breccia 10059 in their comprehensive study of Apollo 11 regolith breccias â€“ their mode is given in the table. They calculated that it had about 24 % highland component, but couldn't directly identify that many clasts of highland rock.

Simon et al. (1984) and Keil et al. (1970) determined the mode for 10059. It contains slightly more of orange glass spheres than the other Apollo 11 breccias (figure 4). Cloud et al. (1970) reported on the size distribution of glass beads (figure 3).

# **Chemistry**

The bulk composition of 10059 was determined by Goles et al. (1970), Smales et al. (1971), Rhodes and Blancahrd (1981) and others (table 1, figures 5 and 6).

Schonfeld and Meyer (1972) calculated that 10059 was a mix of mare basalt with ~16 % gabboic anorthosite and ~1.6 % KREEP, while Rhodes and Blanchard (1981) found it was a mix of soil and high-K basalt. However, Simon et al. (1984) could not identify such a high percentage of highland component.

# **Other Studies**

Cadogen et al. (1972) and Flory et al. (1972) studied carbon compound released from 10059, but apparently never determined the bulk C content!

## **Processing**

Apollo 11 samples were originally described and cataloged in 1969 and "recataloged" by Kramer et al. (1977). There are 7 thin sections of 10059. Note the mismatch between figure 7 and the computer generated processing diagram. Kramer et al. reported that 10059 had been cut by the wire saw.
