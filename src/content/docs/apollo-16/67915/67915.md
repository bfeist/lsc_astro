# **Introduction**

North Ray Crater is about 1 km across and about 230 meters deep, with steep slopes. Lunar sample 67915 was chipped off of Outhouse Rock, which is apparently a piece of House Rock (a large boulder perched on the rim of North Ray Crater, see section on 67935). According to cratering mechanics, material on the rim of a crater should be material from the greatest depth in the crater. One side of 67915 is freshly broken, the other sides are rounded by micrometeorite bombardment (figure 1).

67915 is composed of two main lithologies; both are polymict (Taylor and Mosie 1979). The white breccia (WB) lithology is composed of rounded clasts of finegrained anorthositic breccia, while the gray breccia (GB) lithology is composed of a collection of dark or gray-colored clasts, including soddic ferrogabbro and troctolitic anorthosite. The boundary between the WB and GB lithologies is generally sharp and the WB lithology may be clasts within the GB lithology (figure 3). Both lithologies have about the same composition. 67915 is cemented by shock-melted glass and is also cut with thin black glass veins.

The exposure age of 67915 (~50 m.y.) gives the age of North Ray Crater (Marti et al. 1983, Drozd et al. 1974, Crozaz et al. 1974, Pepin et al. 1974). Crystallization ages of the matix and of the clasts in this rock proved difficult to determine, but are about 3.9 - 4.0 b.y.

# **Petrography**

Weiblen and Roedder (1973) and Roedder and Weiblen (1974), provided the first description of 67915. Nord et al. (1975) found that the matrix was cemented by thin films of glass formed by shock (figure 2). Roedder and Weiblen (1977) studied the thin glass veins in 67915 and discussed their method of formation. Ryder and Norman (1980) give a good summary description.

### **Significant Clasts**

#### Ferroan Anorthosite

Weiblen and Roedder (1973) describe a 3 mm clast with large shocked plagioclase ( $An_{98}$ ) including several small olivine grains ( $Fo_{55-58}$ ).

#### Noritic Anorthosite

Roedder and Weiblen (1974) describe a noritic anorthosite with olivine (Fo<sub>81</sub>) surrounded by orthopyroxene (Wo<sub>3</sub>En<sub>80</sub>) poikilitically enclosing plagioclase (An<sub>93</sub>) (figure 3).

## Sodic Ferro Gabbro -

Weiblen and Roedder (1973), Roedder and Weiblen (1974), Weiblen et al. (1980) and Taylor et al. (1980) describe several clasts of sodic ferro gabbro (see insert for modal analysis). These small clasts all appear to have cataclastic texture and don't have original texture. Plagioclase is sodic (Or<sub>3-9</sub>An<sub>50-60</sub>) and pyroxene is Ferich (En<sub>35</sub>) and exsolved. Attempts to date these clast proved unsuccessful (see below).

#### Granulitic Troctolitic Anorthosite

Sample 67915,67 (figure 9) has a granulitic texture (120 deg. grain boundaries) with  $\sim$ 85% plagioclase (An<sub>95</sub>) and  $\sim$ 15% olivine (Fo<sub>75</sub>) (see also figure 4b in Marti et al. 1983). It has relatively high REE content (analysis given in table 1b).

#### Pristine Troctolitic Anorthosite

Sample 67915,26 (figure 17) is a troctolitic anorthosite with cumulate texture (see figure 4a in Marti et al. 1983). It contains about 85% plagioclase ( $An_{97}$ ) with about 15% intercumulate olivine ( $Fo_{55}$ ).

## Ferro-peridotite

Roedder and Weiblen (1974) illustrate and describe small mafic-rich clasts with Fe-rich, clinopyroxene ( $Wo_{40}En_{45}$ ) and olivine ( $Fo_{62}$ ) with minor plagioclase ( $An_{87}$ ). Some of the pyroxene is coarsely exsolved. They termed this material ferro-peridotite.

#### **Mineralogy**

Note: The composition of minerals in this sample are recorded in an appendix to Roedder and Weiblen.

*Metallic iron:* Misra and Taylor (1975) found that iron grains in 67915 had Co and Ni contents in the meteoritic range.

#### **Chemistry**

Duncan et al. (1973) analyzed the light and dark regions of 67915 and found essentially the same composition (table 1a) for each region. Roedder and Weiblen (1977) found the black glass veins were the same composition as the bulk sample (as determined by Nakamura et al. 1973, Lindstrom and Salpus 1981 and Wanke et al. 1973). Probably the best way to estimate the bulk content is to compare splits with the data on the whole rock by Rancitelli et al. (1973) who determined the K, U, Th for the bulk rock (1180 grams). From this one can see that the main component is anorthositic; the sodic ferrogabbro lithology can only be a minor component!

## Radiogenic age dating

Kirsten et al. (1973), Venkatsan and Alexander (1976), and Marti et al. (1978, 1983) determined Ar/Ar plateau ages for clasts in 67915 (figures 10 - 14). Ages for sodic ferro gabbo were unsuccessful. Plagioclase separates gave the best plateaus  $\sim 3.9$  b.y.

## Cosmogenic isotopes and exposure ages

Behrmann et al. (1973), Marti et al. (1973), Drozd et al. (1974) and Pepin et al. (1974) reported exposure ages of  $48.9 \pm 1.7$  m.y. and  $50.6 \pm 1.5$  m.y.

(respectively) by the <sup>81</sup>Kr method. Pepin et al. calculated <sup>21</sup>Ne = 41.8 m.y. and <sup>38</sup>Ar = 60.3 m.y. Rancitelli et al. (1973) determined the cosmic-ray-induced activity of <sup>22</sup>Na = 34 dpm/kg. and <sup>26</sup>Al = 96 dpm/kg.
